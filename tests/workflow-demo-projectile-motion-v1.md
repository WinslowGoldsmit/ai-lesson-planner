## General Objective

Students will understand the nature of projectile motion as two-dimensional motion under gravity and apply the principle of independence of horizontal and vertical motions to analyze projectile trajectories.

---

## Specific Objectives (Revised Bloom's Taxonomy)

| Level | Objective |
|-------|-----------|
| **Remember** | Students will recall the definition of projectile motion and list at least three real-life examples. |
| **Understand** | Students will explain why horizontal and vertical motions of a projectile are independent of each other. |
| **Apply** | Students will resolve the initial velocity of a projectile into horizontal and vertical components using trigonometry. |
| **Analyze** | Students will compare the acceleration, velocity, and displacement in horizontal and vertical directions and identify the differences. |
| **Evaluate** | Students will assess the validity of the assumptions (uniform gravity, negligible air resistance) for different real-world projectile scenarios. |
| **Create** | Students will sketch and label the trajectory of a projectile launched at an angle, showing velocity components at different points. |

---

* Kinematic equations for uniformly accelerated motion (Class 9–10)
* Resolution of vectors into perpendicular components
* Independence of perpendicular motions
* Trigonometric ratios (sin, cos, tan)

## 5E Instructional Model Lesson Design

### ⏱️ Stage 1: ENGAGE (4 minutes)

**Teacher Activity:**
* Show a short video clip or live demonstration of a ball thrown at an angle, a water fountain arc, or a cricket ball hit for a six.
* Ask: *"Why does the ball follow a curved path instead of a straight line?"*
* Ask: *"What forces are acting on the ball after it leaves the hand or bat?"*

**Student Activity:**
* Observe the demonstration and share initial thoughts.
* Discuss in pairs for 1 minute and volunteer answers.

**Purpose:** Stimulate curiosity and connect to real-life experience.

---

### ⏱️ Stage 2: EXPLORE (6 minutes)

**Teacher Activity:**
* Divide students into pairs. Provide each pair with a small ball (or ask them to use a crumpled paper ball).
* Instruct: *"Throw the ball at different angles — straight up, straight forward, and at 45°. Observe the path."*
* Ask guiding questions: *"Which throw covered the most distance? Which stayed in the air longest?"*

**Student Activity:**
* Perform the quick activity at their desks or in the aisle.
* Record observations mentally or in notebooks.
* Share one observation with the class.

**Purpose:** Allow students to experience projectile motion firsthand and notice patterns.

---

### ⏱️ Stage 3: EXPLAIN (10 minutes)

**Teacher Activity:**
* Define **projectile motion**: *"Motion of an object thrown or projected into the air, moving under the influence of gravity alone."*
* List examples: thrown ball, kicked football, javelin throw, water from a hose, bullet fired from a gun.
* State **assumptions**:
  1. Acceleration due to gravity (g) is constant and downward.
  2. Air resistance is negligible.
  3. Earth is flat over the range of motion (no curvature).
* Draw a diagram on the board: projectile launched at angle θ with initial velocity u.
* **Resolve initial velocity**:
  * Horizontal component: uₓ = u cos θ
  * Vertical component: uᵧ = u sin θ
* Explain **independence of motions**:
  * Horizontal: uniform motion (aₓ = 0, velocity constant)
  * Vertical: uniformly accelerated motion (aᵧ = –g, velocity changes)
* Show the **parabolic trajectory** on the diagram and explain why it occurs.

**Student Activity:**
* Copy the diagram and resolution into notebooks.
* Label the velocity components on their own sketches.
* Ask clarifying questions.

**Purpose:** Build conceptual understanding through direct instruction and visual representation.

---

### ⏱️ Stage 4: ELABORATE (7 minutes)

**Teacher Activity:**
* Present a numerical problem: *"A ball is thrown with a velocity of 20 m/s at an angle of 30° to the horizontal. Find its initial horizontal and vertical velocity components."*
* Walk through the solution on the board:
  * uₓ = 20 × cos 30° = 20 × 0.866 = 17.32 m/s
  * uᵧ = 20 × sin 30° = 20 × 0.5 = 10 m/s
* Ask a follow-up: *"If another ball is thrown at 20 m/s at 60°, which component will be larger? Verify."*
* Briefly discuss: *"At what angle will both components be equal?"* (Answer: 45°)

**Student Activity:**
* Solve the problem along with the teacher.
* Attempt the follow-up independently in notebooks.
* Volunteer to share answers.

**Purpose:** Reinforce learning through guided practice and application.

---

### ⏱️ Stage 5: EVALUATE (3 minutes)

**Teacher Activity:**
* Distribute a quick exit ticket (or ask orally) with two questions:
  1. *"List two assumptions made in projectile motion analysis."*
  2. *"A projectile is launched at 25 m/s, 45° above horizontal. Calculate uₓ and uᵧ."*

**Student Activity:**
* Write answers on the exit ticket or in notebooks.
* Submit before leaving or share with a partner.

**Purpose:** Assess immediate understanding of the lesson's core concepts.

## SECTION C

**1. Answer:**

**(a)** Projectile motion is the motion of an object thrown or projected into the air, moving under the influence of gravity alone, following a curved (parabolic) path. (1 mark)

**Assumptions:** (1 mark)
* Acceleration due to gravity (g) is constant and acts vertically downward
* Air resistance is negligible
* The Earth is flat over the range of motion (no curvature effects)

**(b)** Given: u = 15 m/s, θ = 60°

Horizontal component: uₓ = u cos 60° = 15 × 0.5 = **7.5 m/s** (0.5 marks)

Vertical component: uᵧ = u sin 60° = 15 × 0.866 = **12.99 m/s** (0.5 marks)

**Constant component:** The horizontal component (uₓ) remains constant throughout the flight because there is no horizontal acceleration (aₓ = 0) — no force acts horizontally in ideal projectile motion. (1 mark)

**Changing component:** The vertical component changes continuously because gravity acts vertically downward, causing a constant vertical acceleration (aᵧ = –g). It decreases on the way up, becomes zero at maximum height, and increases in the downward direction on the way down. (1 mark)

---

**2. Answer:**

**(a)** Diagram description: (1.5 marks)
* Draw a projectile launched from origin O at angle θ with initial velocity u
* Show u as a vector at angle θ to the horizontal
* Resolve u into:
  * uₓ = u cos θ (horizontal, along x-axis)
  * uᵧ = u sin θ (vertical, along y-axis)
* Label θ, u, uₓ, uᵧ clearly
* Show parabolic trajectory

Mathematical expressions: (1.5 marks)
* uₓ = u cos θ
* uᵧ = u sin θ

**(b)** Evaluation: (2 marks)

The student's claim is **incorrect**.

**Reasoning:**
The horizontal range formula is R = (u² sin 2θ) / g

For θ = 30°: R₁ = (u² sin 60°) / g = (u² × 0.866) / g

For θ = 60°: R₂ = (u² sin 120°) / g = (u² × 0.866) / g

Since sin 60° = sin 120° = 0.866, **R₁ = R₂**

Therefore, projectiles launched at complementary angles (angles that add up to 90°) with the same initial speed have **equal horizontal ranges**. The student's claim is false.

# COMMON STUDENT MISTAKES

| Mistake | Why It Happens | Correction Strategy |
|---------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Assuming horizontal acceleration exists** | Confusion with vertical motion; students think gravity affects all directions | Emphasize: aₓ = 0 always in ideal projectile motion. Only aᵧ = –g. |
| **Using wrong trigonometric ratio** | Confusion between sin and cos for vertical/horizontal components | Mnemonic: "Y goes with Sine" (vertical → sin, horizontal → cos) |
| **Thinking velocity is zero at maximum height** | Only vertical velocity is zero at peak; horizontal velocity remains | Stress that total velocity is never zero (except for vertical projection) |
| **Believing range depends only on angle** | Forgetting that range depends on both speed and angle | Practice problems with same angle but different speeds |
| **Assuming 90° gives maximum range** | Intuition that "straight up" goes farthest | Derive and show: R_max at θ = 45°, R = 0 at θ = 90° |
| **Ignoring air resistance in real-world analysis** | Over-application of ideal assumptions | Discuss when assumptions break down (light objects, high speeds) |

## Stage 4 – Student Explanation Simplifier

 # Student Explanation Simplifier

## Concept to Explain: Independence of Horizontal and Vertical Motion in Projectile Motion

---

### 1. One-Sentence Explanation

In projectile motion, the horizontal motion keeps going steadily while the vertical motion goes up and down due to gravity — and these two motions do not interfere with each other.

---

### 2. Simple Explanation

Imagine you throw a ball forward while standing on a moving train. To you, the ball goes straight up and down. But to someone standing on the ground, the ball moves forward *and* up and down at the same time.

In projectile motion:
* **Horizontal motion:** The ball keeps moving forward at the same speed because nothing is pushing or pulling it sideways (we ignore air resistance).
* **Vertical motion:** Gravity keeps pulling the ball downward, so it slows down while going up, stops for a moment at the top, and then speeds up while coming down.

The surprising part? These two motions happen **independently**. The ball does not slow down forward just because it is going up. And it does not fall faster forward just because it was thrown harder.

Think of it like two separate stories happening at the same time — one story is about moving forward, and the other is about moving up and down. They share the same ball, but they do not affect each other.

---

### 3. Real-Life Analogy

**The Monkey and the Hunter Story (Simplified)**

Imagine two friends, Raj and Priya, are playing on a flat field.

* Raj stands still and throws a ball straight up.
* At the exact same moment, Priya, standing a few metres away, rolls another ball horizontally along the ground.

Now imagine both balls are somehow lifted off the ground and moving through the air. Raj's ball only goes up and down. Priya's ball only moves forward. 

If Raj throws his ball upward *while also giving it a forward push*, it becomes a projectile. But here is the magic: the "up and down" part behaves exactly as if there were no forward push, and the "forward" part behaves exactly as if there were no upward throw. 

**Another simple analogy:** Imagine you are riding a bicycle and drop a pen from your hand. The pen falls straight down *beside you* — not behind you. Why? Because the pen was already moving forward with you, and that forward motion continues even as it falls. The falling (vertical) and the moving forward (horizontal) are independent.

---

### 4. Step-by-Step Explanation

**Step 1:** A projectile is given an initial push at some angle. This push gives it an initial velocity.

**Step 2:** Break this initial velocity into two parts:
* One part going **forward** (horizontal)
* One part going **upward** (vertical)

**Step 3:** Look at the horizontal part alone:
* No force acts horizontally (ignoring air).
* So horizontal speed **never changes**.
* It is like a ball rolling forever on a perfectly smooth, flat road.

**Step 4:** Look at the vertical part alone:
* Gravity pulls downward constantly.
* So the ball slows down while rising, stops momentarily at the peak, and speeds up while falling.
* This is exactly like throwing a ball straight up in the air.

**Step 5:** Combine both parts:
* At every moment, the ball is at some point because it has moved forward *and* moved up or down.
* The path formed by combining these two separate motions is a **parabola** (a curved path).

**Step 6:** Remember the key rule:
* Change one part (throw harder upward) → only vertical motion changes, horizontal stays the same.
* Change the other part (throw harder forward) → only horizontal motion changes, vertical stays the same.

---

### 5. Common Misconception

**Misconception:** *"If the ball is moving forward fast, it will stay in the air longer."*

**Why it is wrong:** Many students think that throwing a ball harder forward will make it take more time to come down. But time of flight depends **only** on the vertical part (how high it goes), not on how fast it is moving forward.

* A ball thrown very fast horizontally from a height will hit the ground at the **same time** as a ball simply dropped from the same height.
* The fast horizontal ball just travels farther forward before hitting the ground — but it does not stay in the air longer.

**The truth:** Horizontal speed affects *how far* the ball goes (range), not *how long* it stays in the air (time of flight).

---

### 6. Memory Trick

**"H and V Don't See Each Other"**

* **H** = Horizontal
* **V** = Vertical
* They are like two strangers on a train — sitting in the same compartment but not talking to each other.

Or use this rhyme:

> *"Horizontal is calm and steady,*
> *Vertical is where gravity gets ready.*
> *One moves flat, one moves high,*
> *They work together but never ask why."*

**Shortcut for components:**
* **H**orizontal → **H**as **Cos** (Cos starts with 'C', and 'H' comes before 'V' in the alphabet, so Horizontal gets Cos)
* **V**ertical → **V**ery **Sin**ful (Sin starts with 'S', and 'V' comes after 'H', so Vertical gets Sin)

Actually, simpler: **"Y goes with Sine"** — if you draw the angle, the vertical side is opposite, and opposite goes with sine.

---

### 7. Quick Recap

* Projectile motion is a combination of two separate motions: steady forward motion and up-down motion under gravity.
* Horizontal velocity stays constant because no horizontal force acts on the projectile.
* Vertical velocity changes because gravity constantly pulls the projectile downward.
